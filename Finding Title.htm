Lack of adherence to min. control requirements and definition of roles and responsibilities for IAM

According to EMIR RTS 153 Article 4, a CCP shall have adequate internal control mechanisms to assist the board in monitoring and assessing the adequacy and effectiveness of a its risk management policies, procedures and systems.
Moreover, according to BaFin Supervisory Requirements for IT in Financial Institutions (BAIT) 2.2 the management is responsible for ensuring that the regulations governing the IT structure and IT process organization are defined on the basis of the IT strategy and that they are adjusted promptly in the event of changes to activities and processes. It must be ensured that these regulations are implemented effectively. According to CSSF Circular 20/750, point 31Financial institutions should define, document and implement procedures for logical access control (identity and access management). These procedures should be implemented, enforced, monitored and periodically reviewed. The procedures should also include controls for monitoring anomalies

The unit Identity & Access Management (ICA) created the Identity and Access Management Guideline V1.3.2. The aim of this guideline is to define an IAM framework and access handling for DBG. The guideline distinguishes between responsibilities for each IAM topic depending on the onboarding status to SailPoint IIQ (AID760) of the target applications (e.g. in most cases Group Security is accountable/responsible if the asset is onboarded, respectively the asset owner is accountable/responsible if the asset is not onboarded).

Internal Audit (IA) identified the following weaknesses in the control environment of the 1st line of defence (LoD):
Lack of clarity of responsibility to adhere to information security standards issued by Information Security Framework & Governance (ISFG) on topics such as Identity and Access Management (IAM).
Lack of clear definition of roles and responsibilities for execution and monitoring of the above-mentioned control requirements.

As a result of the above-mentioned weaknesses in the control environment, Internal Audit detected several cases where asset owners may not have fully met their responsibilities and this was not identified by the current first line control approach, neither in ICA nor in the affected Product IT teams:
Reconciliation: aim of the reconciliation is to check if authorisation managed/approved in IIQ correctly reflect the actually assigned authorisation in the target systems. Nevertheless, depending on the connector between IIQ and target application, IIQ may not be able to directly analyse the assigned authorisation within the target application. For example, with the Onboard Administrator Active Directory (OAAD) connector, IIQ is only able to reconcile based on the assigned Active Directory workgroups, whereas additional authorisation directly assigned within the target application would not be identified. A control to detect such bypasses is not established.
Usage of central IAM IIQ processes: the onboarding of a target application to IIQ is supported by ICA. After the successful onboarding, the central IAM processes provided by IIQ itself (e.g. provisioning request, recertification etc.) have to be used for/by the target application. Nevertheless, a control (e.g. plausibility check) is not required and not established to monitor if the asset owner of the target application indeed uses the IAM processes provided by IIQ.
Internal Audit noted at least one productive business application which was onboarded for several months without having assigned any users to the onboarded authorisations.
Onboarding status of this business application has been reset during the audit. New planned onboarding finalisation target is February 2023.
Completeness of authorisations: as part of the onboarding process, all available target application authorisation are implemented in IIQ. Nevertheless, no central IAM control (e.g. plausibility check) existed to monitor authorisation added to the target application after onboarding, and not published to IIQ. Internal Audit noted that, for the business application C7 (AID021), a mismatch existed between the onboarded IIQ roles and roles actually available in C7. Internal Audit further noted procedures did not require such control be established.
This was due to a change management process omission (no IIQ update for C7) upon implementing ECC roles in C7 subsequent to the initial C7 onboarding to IIQ in September 2019.
The annual C7 risk assessment process (RAP-3570) started in parallel to our audit, with due date 13 December 2022. It identified the missing ECC roles/business users onboarding to IIQ as a risk and started mitigation activities (completion planned by 30 June 2023). Therefore, Internal Audit issued no additional recommendation. An exceptional risk ticket has been created (ISRM-92912).

Deficiencies in Microsoft M365 Security Incident Management, E-Discovery and Cloud Forensics

Internal Audit (IA) noted for Microsoft M365 Security Response Standard Operating Procedure (SOP) the following weaknesses:
1. Contained inconsistent terminology (e.g. referencing process actors). (SEF-01-01)
2. Lack of an overarching RACI matrix for roles and responsibilities. (SEF-01-01)
3. Contained non-traceable references to sections and other documents. (SEF-01-01)
4. Did not include sufficient details on key concepts such as incident closure criteria, application of incident prioritisation factors or response strategies based on compromise scenarios. (SEF-01-01)
5. Did not mention in the SOP all used metrics, e.g. not TTE (Time to Engage) metrics or TTPIR (Time to Post Incident Review). (SEF-01-01)
6. Did not include incident classification types, e.g. APT or Engineering Bug (SEF-01-01)
7. Inconsistent incident severity definitions in comparison to those used in the incident tracking system, for example 2-Urgent Alert in SOP and 2-Medium in the tool (SEF-06-01)

Further, the following SEF effectiveness deficiencies were identified:
8. Post-Incident Reviews (PIR) were either conducted and closed before the incidents were mitigated or closed or a complete PIR was not conducted, although the incident ticket was marked as having a PIR; instead, only a privacy Root Cause Analysis (RCA) was done, which did not include proper lessons learned or actual post-incident activities. (SEF-03-02)
9. Metrics to track the time between breach declaration and customer notification were not identified. Incorrect values to track Time to acknowledge and missing values to track Time to declare were presented in the dashboards. (SEF-05-01)
10. Information about four GDPR-notifiable incidents (~22% of the total population of 18 GDPR notifiable incidents) was communicated past the SLA of 72 hours (ranging from 73 hours to 352 hours). (SEF-05-01)
11. Time limits (e.g., SLAs, targets, thresholds) were not established for the timeliness of breach declaration once customer impact was confirmed. (SEF-07-01)

The identified items were likely caused by a lack of awareness by the Microsoft process owners of financial institutions regulatory demands.

In case of identified IT risks, appropriate monitoring and management processes shall be set up.

Weaknesses in Googles supply chain management processes

Internal Audit (IA) identified the following gaps within Googles Supply Chain Management, Transparency, and Accountability (STA) process, specifically in the supply chain inventory and supply chain risk management processes.

1. Disclosure of sub-sub-processors
The review has shown that Google does not proactively notify customers regarding identified sub-processing activities beyond the first level of (sub ) processors engaged by Google. Google customers, as data controllers, need to ensure that, in the whole supply chain, only those processors that provide sufficient guarantee to meet the GDPR requirements will process data. Restricting customer information violates external provisions such as GDPR and Googles own Cloud Data Processing Addendum (CDPA) requiring disclosure to data controllers, the entire chain of sub-processors.
Google did not perform an analysis of whether a service provider acts as a sub-processor in line with GDPR and all underlying levels of sub-processing. This requires the same quality of information before the provider gets access to customer data as for the first level of sub-processing. If the provider was not identified as processor in line with GDPR, the need to share information still should have been aligned with the general contractual arrangements for nonmaterial vendors and subcontractors.

2. Incomplete documentation of vendor exit strategies
The review of vendor exit strategies identified documentation completeness shortcomings for four out of five vendor categories, namely Cloud Support, Chronicle, Colocation, Security Guards. E.g. some exit strategies mainly covered non-stressed scenarios, but stressed scenarios were not sufficiently addressed. This impacted the description of the team's resiliency strategy to ensure no disruption to business activities, as well as the documentation of continuity processes to ensure service quality to Google customers. Additionally, the description of alternative solutions/transition plans in stressed scenarios and compliance requirements for exiting a material vendor were found insufficient.

For three out of the four exit strategies with identified shortcomings, the necessary updates have been completed during the audit fieldwork and have been provided to CCAG auditors. Only the update of the exit strategy for Colocation remained outstanding: the stressed exit scenario needed to be reflected within the document.

The root cause was attributed to a lack of governance and guidance during the process execution for de-centralised process activities.

EU, German and Luxembourg regulation requires institutions to have an overview and control over the data processing and involved parties including the strategies to terminate any vendor relationship.

Missing guidance for requirements to perform penetration tests prior to cloud application go-live

Internal Audit identified that seven out of ten sampled applications had not undergone a penetration test prior to going live on Google Cloud Platform (GCP). Section 3.1 of Vulnerability Management Standard v4.0 mandated that penetration tests of all IT Applications and
Infrastructure assets must be conducted upfront before initial go-live, and whenever security relevant changes are released into production.
Security-relevant changes were broadly defined as any change to a system configuration, information content or functionality, which has the potential to change the information security risk status. The underlying infrastructure change would fall under this definition, even when applications were only lift-and-shifted without functional changes to the application code.

Factors that have contributed to this finding include:
▪ Unclear guidance from Group Security (ZZA) as to whether and under which conditions penetration tests would be required for lift
and shift migrations.
▪ Misunderstanding that penetration tests would only be required upon application changes, not infrastructure changes, and consequently assumption that previous penetration tests on the application within the last 12 months would still be valid.
▪ Assumption that Google Cloud Platform Penetration Testing (which was executed once a year) would be sufficient to address the
infrastructure changes.
▪ Assumption that the need for penetration testing would be identified or advised by the Security cross-stream or through the Major Change Process.

German and Luxembourg regulation as noted below require that applications are subject to penetration testing to ensure identification of
vulnerabilities.

Eight Self-Identified Issues (SII), related to deficiencies within DBGs MS Azure environment as reported in the 2023-020 MS Azure Central Services, Security in the Cloud and Vendor Management audit, were expected to be addressed as part of the Hyperion Program. A detailed implementation plan should had been in place by October 2023. The severity ratings of the SIIs were one S3, six S2 and one S1.

Eurex Project Portfolio Management function requires strengthening

The existing Project Portfolio Management (PPM) functions for ECAG and EFAG within the Strategy & Initiatives team (ZDS) should be strengthened. At the time of the audit, ZDS performed only a high-level project monitoring. ZDS included projects in the portfolio that met the financial materiality threshold of 5 million. For EFAG no projects qualified during the audited period and therefore no monitoring took place. For ECAG, the monitoring took place via the monthly jour-fixe meeting in which Clarity one-pagers for each monitored project and the portfolio risk heatmap were collated by ECAG PPM and a brief status update was given verbally in the meeting by each project manager. ECAG PPM also checked for the existence of key project documents. However, ZDS did not perform any controlling functions, only monitoring. Only one individual from each entity was responsible for executing this project-level monitoring.

Further, there was no budget controlling process in place for projects. ZDS only monitored on the basis of a Key Risk indicator (KRI) to determine if an overall project portfolio budget overrun of more than 5% had occurred. For EFAG this KRI monitoring process was not documented. There was no controlling process in place to identify, analyse variances, evaluate their impact, ensure corrective actions were taken and escalate large variances or discrepancies that could not be resolved. Neither was there a process to forecast future costs and potential over/underruns, nor to perform headroom analyses.

The main benefits of an empowered PPM function were not realised. The authority was absent to ensure strategic alignment, optimise resource allocation, assess and manage portfolio risks and provide a structured framework for project management. Specifically, the following important PPM functions were missing from the mandate:
- Strategic alignment: align projects with an organisation's strategic objectives and business goals and ensure that resources are allocated to projects that contribute most to the company's overall strategy and vision.
- Efficient resource allocation: provide visibility into resource availability and utilisation to prevent overburdening of staff and ensure the right people are assigned to the right tasks.
- Portfolio risk management: implement a comprehensive risk management approach at the portfolio level to identify risks that could impact multiple projects or the entire portfolio and assess interdependencies and cumulative risks across projects.
- Governance structure: establish a clear governance framework that defines roles, responsibilities and processes to facilitate transparent and consistent project management processes across the portfolio.

Internal Audit identified the root cause of the above identified weaknesses as an inadequate governance structure.

German and European regulation as well as internal policies require a suitable and transparent organisational structure and adequate internal governance. Internal documentation should include, but not be limited to, a complete and up-to-date description of processes that clearly defines relevant roles, responsibilities, risks and related controls. Accounting standards require businesses to maintain accurate financial records, to faithfully represent the effects of transactions and prepare an analysis of expenses.

SAP contract breach by limitation of agreed audit rights

Deutsche Brse Groups (DBG) key business applications run in SAPs Software as a Service (SaaS) cloud environment are e.g. SAP C/4 HANA (CX), SAP SuccessFactors (SFSF), SAP Business Technology Platform, Enterprise Analytics Cloud, SAP Ariba, SAP Concur or SAP Fieldglass.

Internal Audit (IA) noted that SAP did not properly support DBAG in performing audit activities (e.g. risk assessments, process inspections or control validations) although the contract with SAP granted DBAG unrestricted audit rights. In essence, SAP did not disclose (neither hand-over of documentation nor reading opportunity) any policy document, process, controls, tools, risk data or third-party information, which are essential for DBAGs Third-Party Risk Management.

Specifically, SAP did not disclose the following:
1. Policies, standards, and procedures (e.g. Wiki, Confluence, or WebPage), including e.g. SAP Risk Management Policy, SAP Defensive Penetration Standard or SAP Third Party Security Annex.
2. Internal processes, including e.g. Risk Management, Incident Management or Customer Impact assessment.
3. Internal control frameworks and internal controls on processes, including e.g. Global Security Framework or controls for Risk Management.
4. Internal reporting, including e.g. KPIs on overall SAPs compliance status, vulnerabilities, or incidents.
5. Supporting tools, including e.g. an overview of used tools and linkage to processes, security concepts or risk profiles for each tool such as Compliance Cockpit, Splunk, Brinqa or ServiceNow.
6. Risk data, including e.g. risk inventory, missing patches, vulnerability or penetration test results. SAP refused by stating details regarding the Risk Management topics cannot be shared as they are highly confidential information which is not shared with customers at any time, .
7. Engaged third parties, including e.g. overview of third parties, risk assessment of these third parties or the contractual pass-through of DBAG audit rights.

During the three-day on-site visit, SAP shared information verbally based on standardised, high-level slide decks.

As a result, IA was not in the position to examine SAPs ICT (Information and Communications Technology) Governance and Risk Management. IA could not gain assurance whether the SAP organisation had developed a comprehensive control environment to manage ICT risks, processes, assets, and individuals. This applied to all five Cloud Security Alliance - Cloud Control Matrix control domains in scope: Governance, Risk and Compliance (GRC), Business Continuity Management and Operational Resilience (BCR), Threat & Vulnerability Management (TVM), Security Incident Management, E-Discovery, & Cloud Forensics (SEF), and Supply Chain Management, Transparency, and Accountability (STA).

As a justification, SAP pointed out a strong internal legal advice to treat the audit as a limited customer review for which such detailed requests were not allowed.

After IA triggered DBAG senior management escalation, SAP provided further information and three one-hour policy review sessions for the BCR control domain, delaying audit fieldwork by two weeks. Despite this increased cooperation, SAP did not provide these documents to IA DBAG for review, even though the documents were classified as SAP internal and accessible to SAP employees via SAPs policy portal. At the end of the extended fieldwork, 14 of 41 requests had not been fulfilled at all, most others only partially and still no internal documents after the second document request. SAP also explained that further evidence could not be shared as this would provide an insiders view of how SAP functions and the internal value SAP places on a subset of mission-critical processes.

Ineffectively established audit rights for a critical outsourcing engagement constitutes a violation of regulatory requirements to properly manage the respective third-party risks, monitor the provision of the outsourced activities, and assess the performance and quality of the services provided.

Inappropriate SAP vendor risk management

Internal Audit noted that SAP vendor risk management was not developed to a maturity level which would be considered appropriate in view of the vendors importance. Contributing factors for the conclusion:
- Corporate IT performed a targeted review on SAP in 2023, identifying severe audit right limitations for the following topics: Incident and Data Breach Management, Third Party Provider Management, Malware Management and Penetration Testing.
- DBAG and SAP followed up in working sessions to align on the collaboration and joint understanding for future audits in order to receive the required information. Nevertheless, remediation actions did not result in a sustainably improved DBAG audit support. DBAG was still asked to rely on documents validated by independent certified third parties, e.g. SOC 2 audit reports.
- Corporate IT did not validate the effectiveness of allegedly improved audit support, to confirm the removal of audit right limitations. Further, appropriate risk management measures such as risk entries, risk acceptance, or further escalation were not initiated.

Although IT Security is currently strengthening the Supplier Security Risk process, supported by the Prevalent tool, the contract business owners duty to capture, assess and manage supplier risk remains untouched. Considering SAPs refusal to provide information as outlined in 2024-048_F01, SAP vendor risk management is deemed systematically incomplete.

According to MaRisk, the internal audit function shall examine and assess in a risk-oriented and process-independent manner the effectiveness and appropriateness of the risk management system, as well as the appropriateness of all activities and processes in general, irrespective of whether or not they have been outsourced. For material outsourced activities and processes, the outsourcing contract shall specifically set out appropriate internal auditors rights of information and review.

Lack of and inconsistencies in risk strategies

Internal Audit (IA) noted that EFAG risk strategy, that shall serve as basis for risk management framework and support for EFAG business strategy, was in draft and not yet approved by EFAG Executive Board.
Moreover, IA noted inconsistencies in Business Risk assessments, based on review of the following EFAG and ECAG strategies:
▪
2024 Eurex Corporate Strategy (EFAG Business Strategy) and draft Eurex Frankfurt AG Risk Strategy - In capacity as Exchange Operator for Eurex Deutschland (ED), version 1 (draft EFAG Risk Strategy)
▪
Eurex Clearing Business Strategy 2024 (ECAG Business Strategy) and Eurex Clearing Risk Strategy, version 4.2 (ECAG Risk Strategy)
Namely, in both draft EFAG and approved ECAG Risk Strategy, Business Risk was assessed as not applicable. The assessment resulted from the annual Risk Inventory process and was based on implemented Group risk quantification approach, Risk Appetite Framework and Value-at-Risk (VaR) model. However, this was not in line with the risk assessments available in respective EFAG Business Strategy and ECAG Business Strategy, outlining the Business Risks (Regulatory and Macroeconomic risks) as material.
IA learned that risk assessment in business strategies follows gross approach, meaning that material risks are those that negatively impact entitys financial targets prior to considering operating agreements and transfer of revenues to shareholders. However, no clear rationale for deviations in materiality assessments was provided in strategic and process documents.
Identified issue was caused by incorrect interpretation of regulatory requirements.
In line with external and internal provisions, management must implement appropriate arrangements and systems to identify all significant risks to its operation, and to put in place effective measures to mitigate those risks. Risk strategies should be consistent with business strategies and provide support in reaching strategic goals by identifying, assessing and managing resulting risks.

Non-compliance with encryption policy requirements for CCTV data transmission

Internal Audit (IA) noted that the communication between the video cameras and the Closed-Circuit Television (CCTV) servers hosting the Honeywell Digital Video Manager (DVM) software was not encrypted.



IA identified the root cause of the identified issue as human error/omission.



The DBG CISO Technological Security Standard requires the encryption of data during transmission within the internal network, especially for information classified as strictly confidential or confidential.

In Luxembourg, compliance with these EBA guidelines is enforced by the CSSF, while in Germany, the BaFin has incorporated them into their regulatory framework through the Banking Supervisory Requirements for IT (BAIT), based on the Minimum Requirements for Risk Management (MaRisk).

Additionally, IA observed that the Process Handbook for Visitor Management lacked several critical topics required by the Physical Security Standard (specifically in the section on Physical Site Security & Event Risk Management Reception Services):

The Handbook did not specify the need for receptionist staff to register status changes (e.g., checked-in/checked-out) in the Proxyclick application, which was essential for tracking visitors within the building.

The Handbook did not clarify the receptionists' responsibility to inform visitors about the security requirements and emergency procedures indicated on the back of their badges.

The receptionists were unaware that the emergency procedures were indicated on the back of the visitor badges.

The Handbook failed to address the escorting requirements for DBG data centre server rooms.



Also, the Handbook did not include procedures for handling visitors from law enforcement authorities (e.g., those with search warrants). The Handbook should either incorporate the Guideline for Official Searches procedure developed by Group Legal (GCA) or provide a link to it.



Furthermore, although the Physical Security procedures (i.e., the Process Handbook on Technical Security, the Specific Procedures on site The Square and the Besondere Dienstanweisung fr den Objektschutz und Empfangsdienst in der Liegenschaft der The Cube) outlined several steps and processes for managing non-functioning cameras, they did not explicitly detail a specific process for analysing the risks associated to camera downtimes. A procedure for identifying and implementing risk mitigating controls during such downtimes was not defined.



In addition, several key topics related to visitor management, specifically blacklist procedures and entry/accompany regulations, were not addressed in the procedure titled Besondere Dienstanweisung fr den Objektschutz und Empfangsdienst in der Liegenschaft der The Cube.



Finally, during the 2024-079 CFCL Cork Branch audit, Internal Audit identified a finding relevant to all DBG legal entities: the Process Manual Physical Access Control did not document the existing process for offboarding external contractors.



IA identified the root cause of the identified issues as human error/omission, and lack of awareness of group/cross functional processes.



External and internal provisions require the institution to ensure that business activities are conducted based on organisational guidelines (e.g., manuals, work instructions or workflow descriptions), and that the guidelines are swiftly amended in the event of changes to the activities and processes.

Incomplete ICT Risk Management framework & lack of harmonisation between DBGs first and second LoD

As identified in the CIO Strategy 2021 and the CIO division reorganisation, which was effective from Q3 2022, the closed 2022-051_F01 self-identified issue (SII) planned to establish a consistent Group-wide IT Governance framework and function, including clear delineation of Deutsche Brse Groups (DBG) first (1LoD) and second (2LoD) lines of defence roles and responsibilities, by January 2024.

Internal Audits (IA) review of this SII noted that, although progress was made since 2022, the planned actions for IT Governance framework were incomplete. Due to responsibility shifts triggered by the January 2024 reorganisation and a new written rule structure, formalisation of the ICT Risk Management framework was prolonged. Thus, in the past years, a stable business-as-usual and sustainable state of Deutsche Brse Groups (DBG) ICT Risk Management Framework and Governance had not been achieved. In detail:

1) ICT Risk Management Framework completeness
At the end of 2023, the development of IT Written Rules (ITWR) by 1LoD IT Governance, Risk and Transformation (IRT) was ongoing. However, the January 2024 reorganisation formally shifted responsibility to 2LoD ICT Risk Framework (KJU) for developing 14 Information Communication and Technology (ICT) Guidelines (erstwhile called standards) aligned with the Digital Operational Resilience Act (DORA) risk management framework by December 2024.
1LoD IRT plans to subsequently formalise the ITWR operational documentation (procedures) and fulfil control requirements to verify compliance. This is planned as a multi-year journey.
Although SII 2022-051_F01 anticipated 1LoD IRTs alignment of the IT Governance framework with all affected Shared and Product ITs, this was not ensured since the alignment was on a best effort basis.

Moreover, the priority-oriented DORA-readiness implementation plan scheduled completion of Design (DE) and Operating Effectiveness (OE) for 7 out of 18 areas by January 2025. For the remaining eleven areas, prioritisation efforts were channeled towards achieving maturity, with DE completed by that date, and continuous improvements towards OE scheduled to last until end of 2025. The DBG Legal Entities DORA progress was tracked and monitored by appropriate oversight mechanisms, e.g. Steering Committees, Board presentations.

2) DBG 1LoD and 2LoD responsibilities
By end of 2023, DBGs 2LoD lacked an IT framework and performed no IS Compliance control activities. Following the 1 January 2024 reorganisation, 2LoD defined a target IT Compliance & ICT Operating Model (TOM) to include the missing activities. By June 2024, it was only aligned with DBAG, ongoing with other Legal Entities (LEs).

IA identified the following 1LoD and 2LoD roles and responsibilities gaps regarding ICT activities, as well as alignment between 2LoD functions of affected LEs:
After the responsibilities split/handover in Q1 2024 between 1LoD IRT and 2LoD CICTRO/CISO (ICT), co-ordination role for ICS (Internal Control Systems) in DBAG IT has been assigned to 2LoD CICTRO/CISO, as well as control monitoring and quality assurance activities. Since providing guidance on how controls are defined and executed would impact 2LoD CICTRO independence to oversee 1LoD activities, 2LoD CICTRO was aiming at coordination without providing guidance. However, a transfer of responsibility to IRT on providing guidance to 1LoD was also not initiated.
Pending LE CISOs (2LoD) decision to include performing oversight and controlling of IT/ICT risks by either extending their responsibilities or to assign it within other 2LoD. Agreement with DBAG CICTRO/CISO over the potential outsourcing services to follow.
Pending LEs Compliance Officers decision on aligning with DBAG (2LoD) Group Compliance Strategy (SCG) regarding Group-wide IT Compliance setup and responsibilities, focusing on design-type controls and reviews of ICT or IS regulations. This was due to ongoing update of the new Group Compliance SDS (Service Definition Statement) and the IT Compliance services to be provided to LEs. Therefore, the agreement on the services between DBAGs SCG and LEs, e.g. CBL (incl. CS/CI/LuxCSD) and CFCL was delayed. (For details, refer section 6.2)

3) IS Assurance plan completeness and Security Risk Management
Incomplete 2LoD oversight in the past years DBAG and impacted LEs (dependent on DBAGs coverage) IS assurance plans execution was partial due to approach changes or personnel unavailability. (For details, refer section 6.3).
The Ecosystem Security Risk Management (ESRM), managed via the Prevalent (AID2040) tool, did not have a sufficient solution to record affected LEs risk acceptance approvals. The current provision is to inform the using LEs about the risk/status and only provide the owning LEs confirmation/acceptance evidence. This deficiencys remediation is being tracked via IAs open Audit BaFin IT Outsourcing 2022 validation report Note #N10.

The above-mentioned weaknesses were caused by an inadequate governance structure. It was also impacted by personnel / organisational structure changes throughout 2024.

German and Luxembourg regulation emphasises the need for robust governance arrangements and adequate internal control mechanisms. Processes, as well as the related tasks, competencies, responsibilities, controls and reporting channels should be clearly defined and coordinated. Regulation also requires that activities are conducted based on organisational guidelines.

To note, the remediation measures for above-mentioned activities are subject to multiple team coordination and availability, including the appointment of the CICTRO/CISO personnel. Thus, submission to IA of the Corrective Action Plan (CAP) is planned by 24 January 2025.

DBG DC room video monitoring The DCs closed-circuit television (CCTV) video monitoring of the DBG-rented DC rooms only covered entry and exit points. Other DC room areas were not CCTV-monitored, creating blind areas particularly for the critical IT infrastructure aisles with business-critical data. These DC rooms are technical rooms facilitating operation of DBGs IT infrastructure. Any unescorted and escorted person could cause intentional or unintentional damage to unmonitored ICT systems. IA was informed the same video monitoring approach was in place in all DBG DCs, including those not inspected (Equinix in Frankfurt Seckbach/Germany and CBL-owned in Kirchberg/Luxembourg).

The auditors observed onsite that these critical infrastructure components were also left without further monitoring during maintenance activity. NTT confirmed the compliance to their common standards which would include CCTV monitoring and escorting of maintenance work, which remained in contradiction to the auditors observations.

DC pool-badge (visitor) management process DBGs NTT DC physical security concept for authorised staff relied on three-step authentication: electronic key (badge), unique pin-code and biometric authentication (hand scan). As a result, instead of e.g. concrete barriers, pollards or rocks to stop vehicles, the only protection was a simple metal chain-link fence. IAs interview of NTT confirmed their risk assessment did not include this area. NTT confirmed later they did not see the need for anti-ram protection in the absence of related incidents in the past and would only install such protections for DIN EN 50600 certified DCs, which DBG had not required.

Anti-ram protection The NTT DC area perimeter and physical barrier inspection identified a vulnerability resulting from the neighbouring area, a car park and garages close to DC rooms. The NTT DC was built very close to the neighbouring area, only leaving 3-4 metres between the building and the fence, allowing no further safeguard distance. As a result, instead of e.g. concrete barriers, pollards or rocks to stop vehicles, the only protection was a simple metal chain-link fence. IAs interview of NTT confirmed their risk assessment did not include this area. NTT confirmed later they did not see the need for anti-ram protection in the absence of related incidents in the past and would only install such protections for DIN EN 50600 certified DCs, which DBG had not required.

DORA article 28 and the EBA Guidelines on ICT and security risk management (EBA/GL/2019/04) ask financial institutions identify and manage their ICT and security risks. The ICT function(s) should manage all risks identified within the financial institutions risk appetite limits and in compliance with external and internal requirements. Policies, procedures and controls shall limit physical or logical access to information assets and ICT assets.

Weaknesses in Default Management test plan

Internal Audit (IA) observed that the Credit Risk & Default Management (DCZ) team independently determined the scope of the annual default management test plan. Given the nature of the default management process, many teams within the company are involved in the process in various capacities, particularly in the liquidation of the inherited portfolio, such as executing and settling trade instructions. Since the default of a clearing member is an uncommon event, many of the corresponding procedures triggered only in a default event have not been executed or tested. Without this coordination, operational readiness in case of a clearing member default cannot be ensured.



EU regulations mandate that central counterparties regularly conduct simulations (tests) of clearing member default scenarios. These tests are crucial to ensure the CCPs and its clearing members operational readiness, identify bottlenecks and improve procedures for liquidating defaulted portfolios.

Ineffective purchasing processes and controls

The audit of the Purchasing process revealed a range of weaknesses related to inconsistencies in process and control design, gaps in control execution and process inefficiencies. These issues mostly affect core purchasing processes including the Source-to-Contract, Purchase-to-Pay and Car Fleet Management processes. Minor gaps were noted in the Supplier Management process. Internal Audit (IA) noted that purchasing process documentation lacks overall clarity, completeness and accuracy, further impacting operational consistency and effectiveness.

IA noted the following key issues related to control design and operating effectiveness:

Weaknesses in the control framework related to inconsistent frequency and allocation of controls in the Purchasing Manual, SAP GRC ICS and control documentation, leading to misalignment with actual practices. The majority of controls were executed at least twice, ad hoc and periodically as compliance check. Further, clear guidelines for sample-based purchasing controls including responsibilities, segregation of duties and follow-up activities, were lacking. This approach, combined with a high volume of purchasing operations, limited process efficiency.
Unclear instructions on specific process steps and control applicability in different purchasing tools, dispersed across the Purchasing Manual and related tool-specific guidelines. This led to misunderstanding of the process flow and associated controls.
Other minor deficiencies in the Purchasing Manual regarding inaccurate and outdated flow-charts, unclear descriptions of contract call-offs applicability, goods receipt confirmation and inaccuracies concerning access right checks conducted for internally managed catalogues. Inaccuracies related to Supplier Governance Committee existing for ECAG, CBF, Eurex Repo, and DBAG, but not Clearstream Holding. Also, there were gaps in SAP GRC ICS as the Car Fleet Management process was not included in the Process Map and there were no entries for issue management and escalation, relating to supplier management.
Inadequate controls over Waivers of Competitive Bid (Bid Waiver) as Corporate Purchasing (FPU) did not ensure completeness and accuracy of all Bid Waiver requests prior to approval. In addition, there was a lack of automated controls disabling submission of incomplete digital Bid Waiver request forms and ensuring approval solely by Board Members in specific cases.
Limited effectiveness of Source-to-Contract and Purchase-to-Pay process caused by lack of operational consistency, missing process and control documentation. On a sample of 18 Purchase Orders with 24 transactions IA Identified gaps related to lack of sourcing documentation in one case, unclear rules regarding quantitative evaluations of bids and savings calculation. Also, one control over Purchase Order Modifications was not implemented.
Lastly, IA noted that only one fully trained FTE possessed the necessary expertise to perform complex data analytics and reporting tasks. There was no risk identified in relation to the continuation of the core purchasing processes, however, this lead to Internal Audit experiencing delays in receiving the requested data and performing timely audit testing. This resource constraint further highlights the need for increased knowledge transfer and resource resilience in FPU.
Overall, redundant and repetitive controls created confusion, with negative impact on the control environment, process efficiency and availability of accurate audit evidence.
In line with external and internal provisions, an institution should maintain adequate arrangements, safeguards and controls to ensure an effective internal control system and risk management. Business activities must be conducted based on up-to-date written procedures, with clearly defined roles, responsibilities, risks and related controls. Moreover, control activities need to be defined, documented and presented in a way that is understandable by third parties.

Weaknesses in the Terms of Reference (ToR) for the Supplier Governance Committee (SGC)

In 2021, the Supplier Governance Committee (SGC) was established as an advisory body for the selection of supplier relationships and review of supplier risks for Corporate Purchasing and the corresponding Terms of Reference (ToR) for DBAG, CBF, ECAG and ExR were prepared. These were last updated in the context of the introduction of the Supply Chain Due Diligence Act (LkSG) effective January 2023. No specific internal requirements for voluntary committees exist.
As Committee members, the Terms of Reference (ToR) defines several function takers of first and second Lines of Defense (LoD) in Chapter 6.1. Each Committee member has one vote and thus has a direct influence on supplier onboarding decisions. Internal Audit is nominated as a non-voting guest on a permanent basis. Overall guests have no voting rights.

Internal Audit noticed that the ToR for the SGC was lacking:
clear policies and mechanisms to avoid conflicts of interest
a regular rotation of committee members
verification and disclosure of members regarding direct financial or personal interests in the supplier
In addition, the distribution/participants list for the Legal entity specific Supplier Governance Committee (SGC) was outdated and did not comply with the defined function takers of first and second LoD (e.g. the Human Rights Officer as responsible under LkSG for monitoring risk management within the company was not part of any of the distribution list for DBAG, CBF, ECAG or ExR).

The DBG Business Code of Conduct defines DBG's conduct and ethical principles. As "tone at the top", they form the benchmark for entrepreneurial decisions and entrepreneurial action. Together, with internal compliance guidelines, particularly the DBG Policy on Conflicts of Interest, the expectations of the behavior of all managers and employees as well as business practices are addressed to ensure compliance with the values and principles of the DBG.

Lacking definition and action for identified supplier issues or incidents

The Purchasing Manual contained mitigating actions based on the risk score for identified issues or incidents with a supplier. If the risk score was yellow, a remote audit should be conducted.

Internal Audit noted that this remote audit requirement was not fulfilled. The reporting for the second half of 2024 contained five suppliers with a yellow risk score and no related remote audits had been conducted.

In addition, the Purchasing Manual stated that the Supplier Governance Committee (SGC) decides about measures for red risk scores. SGC exists for ECAG, CBF, Eurex Repo, and DBAG. IA noted that for all other legal entities without an SGC, the procedure related to red risk scores was not defined.

In this context, Corporate Purchasing (FPU) represented that so far, a red classification had never occurred.

In line with German and Luxembourg regulation and DBG policy, business activities must be conducted based on organizational guidelines. They should include a complete and up-to-date description of processes that clearly defines relevant roles, responsibilities, risks and controls.

Serious weaknesses in the ICS Framework and its implementation impairing the ICS Effectiveness

The Internal Control System (ICS) framework is aimed at managing risks on a process level, preventing material losses, achieving Deutsche Brse Groups (DBG or Group) corporate goals/business strategy and safeguarding its continued existence. Information on the processes, risks and controls is used, among others, by second- and third-line functions.

Internal Audit (IA) identified the following weaknesses in the ICS framework:
- insufficient assurance on effectiveness of internal controls, and
- lack of correlation in risk assessment and undefined mandatory fields, which hinder execution of Quality Assurance activities.

Insufficient assurance on effectiveness of internal controls of key risk and their controls:
The Executive Boards and local ICS experts regularly informed relevant legal entity Supervisory Boards and the Audit Committees, as well as some regulatory authorities (if relevant), about the internal control systems effectiveness. Overall ICS effectiveness confirmation was provided to boards based on:
- Process Owners and/or Process Operators self-assessments of overall control design and operating effectiveness during the sign-off phase, including for processes with high and medium risks;
- results of internal and external audits;
- local ICS experts Quality Assurance check results (3-year cycle).

IA noted that across all legal entities in scope of the audit 62% of internal controls were assessed as key controls. Given the high volume of key controls, only a limited assurance on its effectiveness was provided. Self-assessments represented the majority of these inputs. IA noted those were not based on any documented testing by the Process Owners/Operators. This did not ensure that, in particular, the high risks and their control effectiveness inputs were adequate.

Furthermore, also the local ICS experts dedicated, risk-based Quality Assurance (QA) checks included a quality review of data inserted into the ICS and an assessment of control design. However, these checks did not involve any operating effectiveness testing. Evidence of control performance was also not required. There was therefore no mandatory verification that controls were functioning as intended, hampering ability to promptly identify and address control weaknesses. For most processes with lower overall ratings (typically low and negligible risk), QA checks may be conducted less frequently (e.g. once in three years) and with limited scope, without validating either control design or operating effectiveness. Placing assurance on overall DBG or individual legal entitys ICS adequacy and effectiveness without performing appropriate, risk-based QA activities on internal control effectiveness was considered inadequate.

Furthermore, while the ICS framework outlined a concept of key vs standard internal controls, there were no clear definitions of what a key control was and implications of classifying a control as either key or standard.

Due to regulatory requirements, CSS entities have implemented a dedicated Risk & Control Assurance (LOD) as an independent unit, aimed to effectively assure senior management of CSS entities, that the control environment effectively manages risks or detects and remediates deficiencies. Similarly, other legal entities have established (e.g. CFCL, CFCS, CFCHK, LuxCSD) or intend to establish dedicated functions (e.g. CAL), whose responsibility includes testing the operating effectiveness of internal controls. For other legal entities where no similar functions/roles with this type of responsibility are present, no assurance can be provided about most of the ICS key controls adequacy and effectiveness. Additionally, CBL, CFCL and CFCS benefit from ISAE control assurance activities conducted by the external consultant, PricewaterhouseCoopers, which offer additional comfort about a qualitative control environment and validate the proper execution of 1st LoD controls. Nevertheless, considering the large number of key controls, the dedicated functions or external consultants are unable to test and validate operating effectiveness of all key controls.

IA also identified further areas for ICS framework improvement, which hindered ICS data QA activities execution for all legal entities in scope of the audit:

The current ICS framework focused on process-level individual risk assessment per single risk and did not define the correlation between a processs risk assessment and its underlying sub-processes risk assessments. IA noted that in 109 cases (2% of total population), a processs Inherent risk rating was lower than all its sub-processes Inherent risk ratings. On the contrary, in 801 cases (7% of total population), the processs rating was unexplainably higher than all its sub-processes.

IA acknowledges that the ICS framework positioned the process risk assessment as a purely indicative field, aimed to support individual risk assessment. It did not feed any reporting on the ICS in general or any ICS components. However, as process risk assessment fields were treated as purely indicative, IA noted they were not maintained adequately, resulting in same sub-processes Inherent risk rating inconsistencies.

The current ICS framework did not explicitly outline, which Process Map and Risk Control Matrix (RCM) fields should be treated as mandatory, which left room for different interpretations by readers, including various by ICS Experts in different Legal entities. As a result, up to 4% of all fields were left blank in Process Maps and RCMs. Three specific Process Map fields were left blank in 32% of all cases.

Serious weaknesses in the ICS Framework and its implementation impairing the ICS Effectiveness

The Internal Control System (ICS) framework is aimed at managing risks on a process level, preventing material losses, achieving Deutsche Brse Groups (DBG or Group) corporate goals/business strategy and safeguarding its continued existence. Information on the processes, risks and controls is used, among others, by second- and third-line functions.

Internal Audit (IA) identified the following weaknesses in the ICS framework:
- insufficient assurance on effectiveness of internal controls, and
- lack of correlation in risk assessment and undefined mandatory fields, which hinder execution of Quality Assurance activities.

Insufficient assurance on effectiveness of internal controls of key risk and their controls:
The Executive Boards and local ICS experts regularly informed relevant legal entity Supervisory Boards and the Audit Committees, as well as some regulatory authorities (if relevant), about the internal control systems effectiveness. Overall ICS effectiveness confirmation was provided to boards based on:
- Process Owners and/or Process Operators self-assessments of overall control design and operating effectiveness during the sign-off phase, including for processes with high and medium risks;
- results of internal and external audits;
- local ICS experts Quality Assurance check results (3-year cycle).

IA noted that across all legal entities in scope of the audit 62% of internal controls were assessed as key controls. Given the high volume of key controls, only a limited assurance on its effectiveness was provided. Self-assessments represented the majority of these inputs. IA noted those were not based on any documented testing by the Process Owners/Operators. This did not ensure that, in particular, the high risks and their control effectiveness inputs were adequate.

Furthermore, also the local ICS experts dedicated, risk-based Quality Assurance (QA) checks included a quality review of data inserted into the ICS and an assessment of control design. However, these checks did not involve any operating effectiveness testing. Evidence of control performance was also not required. There was therefore no mandatory verification that controls were functioning as intended, hampering ability to promptly identify and address control weaknesses. For most processes with lower overall ratings (typically low and negligible risk), QA checks may be conducted less frequently (e.g. once in three years) and with limited scope, without validating either control design or operating effectiveness. Placing assurance on overall DBG or individual legal entitys ICS adequacy and effectiveness without performing appropriate, risk-based QA activities on internal control effectiveness was considered inadequate.

Furthermore, while the ICS framework outlined a concept of key vs standard internal controls, there were no clear definitions of what a key control was and implications of classifying a control as either key or standard.

Due to regulatory requirements, CSS entities have implemented a dedicated Risk & Control Assurance (LOD) as an independent unit, aimed to effectively assure senior management of CSS entities, that the control environment effectively manages risks or detects and remediates deficiencies. Similarly, other legal entities have established (e.g. CFCL, CFCS, CFCHK, LuxCSD) or intend to establish dedicated functions (e.g. CAL), whose responsibility includes testing the operating effectiveness of internal controls. For other legal entities where no similar functions/roles with this type of responsibility are present, no assurance can be provided about most of the ICS key controls adequacy and effectiveness. Additionally, CBL, CFCL and CFCS benefit from ISAE control assurance activities conducted by the external consultant, PricewaterhouseCoopers, which offer additional comfort about a qualitative control environment and validate the proper execution of 1st LoD controls. Nevertheless, considering the large number of key controls, the dedicated functions or external consultants are unable to test and validate operating effectiveness of all key controls.

IA also identified further areas for ICS framework improvement, which hindered ICS data QA activities execution for all legal entities in scope of the audit:

The current ICS framework focused on process-level individual risk assessment per single risk and did not define the correlation between a processs risk assessment and its underlying sub-processes risk assessments. IA noted that in 109 cases (2% of total population), a processs Inherent risk rating was lower than all its sub-processes Inherent risk ratings. On the contrary, in 801 cases (7% of total population), the processs rating was unexplainably higher than all its sub-processes.

IA acknowledges that the ICS framework positioned the process risk assessment as a purely indicative field, aimed to support individual risk assessment. It did not feed any reporting on the ICS in general or any ICS components. However, as process risk assessment fields were treated as purely indicative, IA noted they were not maintained adequately, resulting in same sub-processes Inherent risk rating inconsistencies.

The current ICS framework did not explicitly outline, which Process Map and Risk Control Matrix (RCM) fields should be treated as mandatory, which left room for different interpretations by readers, including various by ICS Experts in different Legal entities. As a result, up to 4% of all fields were left blank in Process Maps and RCMs. Three specific Process Map fields were left blank in 32% of all cases.

CBF - 17 processes (11 desirable and 6 necessary)
CBL - 26 processes (9 desirable and 17 necessary)
CFCL - 10 processes (4 essential, 4 desirable and 2 necessary)

a new initiative is being introduced, which will result in automatic termination of the Mini-master record when the valid until date arrives.

Significant deficiencies in third-party lifecycle management and data governance

Various financial industry regulations demand a consistent and coherent approach that spans the entire lifecycle of third-party relationships, from initial risk assessment and onboarding through contract management, operational oversight, incident handling, and exit strategies. An effective third-party risk management requires clear traceability of responsibilities, harmonized documentation and IT applications as well as integrated risk controls across all relevant functions and DBG legal entities.

The DBG outsourcing framework has recently been updated to include ICT third-party risk management. This extended the already complex outsourcing and third-party management processes, which were already fragmented, siloed and lacked alignment and harmonization. DBGs third-party management activities were spread across many legal entities, departments as well as multitudinous Contract- and Business Owners. Impact could be exemplified by inconsistently applied third-party management, not aligned roles and responsibilities, inconsistent and decentralized due diligence, risk assessment and monitoring across the 1st LoD, e.g. ownership for third-party management or contract management, ineffective third-party oversight or missing data governance. Illustrative risk drivers include:
a) Multiple teams within DBG (Purchasing, Business Owners, Ecosystem Partner Risk Management, IT Strategic Provider Management) conducted due diligence, risk assessment, and monitoring activities using different tools and methodologies. This led to redundancy, inefficiency and inconsistent results. Central oversight and consolidation of inputs from different internal stakeholders were not established.
b) Lack of robust data governance for contract and third-party data resulted in inconsistent, incomplete or inaccurate entries across multiple tools and repositories for contract and third-party data. Third parties were not timely, unambiguous and reliably identifiable. Comprehensive controls to ensure the completeness and accuracy of the contract inventory and other third-party data did not exist.
c) DBG legal entities did not establish roles and processes to provide coordinated management of and communication with third parties. The relationship management was set up contract based, resulting in hundreds of contract Business Owners across the organization performing duplicated tasks and actions, leading to unstructured and fragmented third-party documentation. Consequently, the missing coordination resulted in third parties like SAP and Microsoft complaining about repeated and uncoordinated information requests in 2025.
d) A detailed vendor management review of a sample of six major ICT third parties (Google, Tata, Bottomline, Equinix, NTT and Appway) revealed that DBG had not implemented a fully aligned third-party governance, risk or control framework according to the obligations laid down in contracts, including the DORA contractual annex. As DBG works with several hundred ICT third parties plus more than ten third parties supporting CIF, DBGs internal deadline for implementation and operationalization by December 2025 might be unfeasible in this regard.
e) Although deficiencies in the operationalization of the third-party risk management frameworks were self-identified (see SII-1 below), specific support has not been received by Contract/ Business Owners. Assurance was missing that DORA third-party risk management and vendor management specific expertise were available at the Business Owners.

A full list of supporting examples from 2025-033 and 2025-026 audits fieldwork is included in Appendix B.

A comprehensive, end-to-end governance approach for Third-parties management is a regulatory imperative. Fragmented, unaligned and siloed processes, particularly at the 1st LoD, impact 2LoD oversight and are explicitly discouraged, as they undermine the resilience, transparency, consistency and accountability that, particularly, DORA seeks to enforce across the financial sector.

Internal Audit (IA) noted that for the month of January 2025, capped amount for the committed credit lines from DZ Bank was 333m, which was 27% of the sum of committed credit lines of 1,228m. Therefore, the 25% limit was breached.

Upon inquiries, auditees confirmed that their calculation of the concentration also considered the available FX Swap lines with Toronto Dominion Bank, hence per their methodology, the credit line with DZ Bank remained below 25% limit. Given EMIR is not 100% clear on which kind of credit lines should be included, the FX swap lines were included in the concentration capping algorithm some years ago. However, the methodology applied is not consistent with the requirements set out within the Methodology Concept.

EMIR Article 44 defines that one Clearing Member (group) may only provide 25% of all committed credit lines considered in the regulatory required scenarios, in particular within the control relevant Cover-2 scenario. Thus, the amounts of the credit lines may need to be capped for Liquidity Stress Testing, depending on the overall amount of available committed credit lines provided by Clearing Members.

Liquidity Stress Testing Methodology Concept Chapter 3.2.1 explains that the maximum amount considered per single commercial bank is 25% of the total committed uncollateralized credit lines. If a single commercial bank provides more than 25% of the overall uncollateralized credit lines, the exceeding amount is excluded in the liquidity stress testing calculation.

Absence of a comprehensive DBG data leakage prevention concept to protect critical data assets

DBG lacks a data leakage prevention (DLP) concept as foundation for structured, comprehensive and risk-based protection against data leakage. As a consequence, the measures to reduce data leakage risk implemented at DBG have not been derived from a defined DLP concept and, in their entirety, do not form an effective DLP capability.

Specifically, DBG failed to:
Establish DLP-specific data classification, especially defining and identifying its most critical data (data crown jewels). The way application-level information classification was implemented does not enable and support identification of data crown jewels in DBG. Also, it does not consider multiple possible criteria such as critical and important functions and competition-critical importance. Furthermore, existing document classification (labeling) measures are independent from data criticality and are not consistently enforced for manual processes involving data extracts such as IDPs.
Conduct a risk assessment to comprehensively define and identify data exfiltration points and establish leakage scenarios against which this data needs protection.
Define risk metrics/requirements catalogue for safeguarding data from being leaked depending on its criticality.
Determine suitable technical preventive controls based on a comprehensive DLP concept.
Implement effective oversight measures to monitor control effectiveness and detect breaches.

In detail, Internal Audit identified several gaps in data classification, safeguarding and data leakage risk management:

Data classification and safeguarding
DBG has not defined, identified, or inventoried its most sensitive data assets ("data crown jewels"). Also, there is no comprehensive requirements catalogue for safeguarding data from leakage based on criticality. This lack of a top-down approach prevents focused controls on the highest risks associated with potential data leakage of the most critical data.
The implemented information classification (bottom-up approach) did not result in a manageable scope of IT systems containing the most critical data. The information classification is not consistently derived from considerations of key dimensions e.g., critical and important functions, personally identifiable information (PII) or data supporting strategic decisions. As of July 1, 2025, 279 IT applications were classified with the highest confidentiality level (critical), requiring the strictest data leakage controls across a broad scope of targets.
2LoD ICT Risk Management has not defined the information classification risk levels (Critical/Major/Minor/Negligible). These were only defined in the 1LoD (Group Security) written rules.

Data leakage risk management
The DLP control system lacks a foundation in a comprehensive, systematic, and documented risk assessment, failing to adequately address two key aspects:
- Data leakage paths: The current IT application-based risk assessment lacks a strategic DLP approach, resulting in incomplete identification and management of key data leakage risks. For example, some file types in use (e.g., .csv) lack classification requirements; email blocking controls are limited to Excel and strictly confidential files only and can easily be circumvented. See also finding 2025-099_F04 for further details.
- Data processing risks outside dedicated IT tools: Due to limited system functionality or lacking customer/service/user features, many business processes rely on manual data handling outside of dedicated IT tools e.g., within IDPs. This lack of system integration prevents effective data flow controls, increasing the risk of errors and malicious activity, especially when data is shared externally via email. Also, reliance on IDP solutions and manual activities introduces an additional risk layer. Recent data leakage incidents demonstrated this, with IDP malfunction identified as one of the root causes. This lack of resilience and scalability creates multiple points of failure in the business processes, particularly under pressure. Strategic DLP approaches and risk assessments must specifically address these vulnerabilities.

European, German and Luxembourg regulation and guidance require financial entities continuously identify all ICT risk sources. In accordance with their ICT risk management framework, they shall minimize the ICT risk impact by deploying appropriate strategies, policies, procedures, protocols and tools. Appropriate technical and organizational measures shall be implemented to ensure a level of security appropriate to the risk. The IT systems, the related processes and other information domain elements shall ensure data confidentiality.

Fragmentation within the existing incident management coverage & operationalization for data leakage

While the existence of the ICT incident management procedure is acknowledged, Internal Audit identified fragmentation within the existing incident management coverage and operationalization for data leakage, and therefore the need for enhancement and harmonization across the Group:
Incident scope definitions varied significantly between functions, with overlapping and sometimes conflicting categorizations that hindered a unified understanding of incident types. As a result, depending on the point where an incident is identified in the organization, the incident response may take a different route involving different stakeholders in the absence of a central single-entry point for all incident types

Gaps in DLP controls for blocking outbound emails with attachments

Internal Audit (IA) specifically reviewed the data leakage prevention (DLP) environment for email as the most used path for outbound communication, which played a central role in recent Clearstream data leakage incidents. IA identified the following gaps in controls for email DLP enforcement and exception management, leading to weaknesses in blocking outbound emails with attachments, especially unlabeled ones:
Email DLP is based on rules to block outgoing emails depending on their labeling. Hence, unlabeled email attachments can be sent out to external recipients and will not be blocked. This topic is also covered in the ongoing Ameli project (Tz. 170/171 CEU/ECAG). However, only a limited set of file types, i.e., Microsoft Office and PDF, are required to be labeled.
The new DLP rule for blocking non-labeled Microsoft Office and PDF files, planned to go live in June 2025, has not been implemented yet due to a bug within Office365.
As a first reaction to the recent data leakage incidents, Excel attachments have been blocked by default from being sent out externally starting April 2025. However, about 1,200 exceptions have been maintained in DBGs IIQ central access rights management system for employees of different legal entities, allowing them to send out Excel attachments to external recipients. In addition, such exceptions have been maintained for about 280 shared mailboxes. This gap has been addressed during the audit as part of the Clearstream initiative for enhancing email DLP.
In general, it is possible to circumvent email DLP controls by renaming file extensions and by changing the label. This gap will be addressed as part of the 2025-099_F01 finding.
On the intranet information page Classification and Labeling it was incorrectly stated that emails with unlabeled attachments will not be sent. This gap has been addressed during the audit.
